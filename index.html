Introduction

   When an object surpasses the speed of sound, sound waves emitted by the aircraft pile up and form one single shockwave. 
   This results in the sonic boom. This sonic boom is the reason why non-military aircraft cannot exceed the speed of sound 
   over land, as the shockwave generated can cause structural damage and disrupt wildlife. However, new approaches to aircraft 
   design and Mach cutoff physics are leading the way to supersonic aircraft producing a low or negligible boom.

The X-59

   The X-59 is an example of a low boom aircraft. It achieves this by its unusual design. The most obvious feature of this 
   aircraft is the nose of the plane, which stretches 11m and is roughly one third of the plane’s length. This long nose helps
   break up the shockwaves that would form a sonic boom with outer mould lines that help separate the shockwaves. Because the 
   X-59 has such a long nose, the cockpit it positioned in the middle of the aircraft and has no forward-facing windows to help 
   reduce air drag. Instead, the X-59 has 4k cameras that produce a live feed of what is in front of the aircraft. Additionally,
   the engine sits on top of the wings so the sound waves produced by the engine aren’t pointing towards the ground, further 
   minimising the amount of sound waves that can reach the ground. Another feature of the X-59 is that it is coated with antistatic
   paint, to avoid static electricity buildup on the aircrafts surface, which could affect electrical equipment. 
   
   The X-59 is yet to go for its first test flight, as NASA are currently only testing the aircraft on the ground. However, there are 
   plans for the X-59 to operate at a lower altitude around 240mph, before eventually building up to supersonic speeds.

The XB-1

          
   The XB-1 is an example of an aircraft that uses Mach cutoff physics to produce inaudible waves at ground level
   The XB-1 has actually undergone test flights- unlike the X-59, and went supersonic for the first time on 28 January
   and on the 10th Febuary broke the sound barrier three times during the flight without an audible boom reaching the ground.
   (https://boomsupersonic.com/flyby/xb-1-live-blog-flight-test-program#flight-13). The XB-1 achieved this by its boomless cruise
   and its Symphony engines- which allow enhanced transonic performance, allowing the XB-1 to go supersonic above 30,000 feet. 

   Boomless cruise utilises well-known Mach cutoff physics, where a sonic boom refracts upwards due to temperature and wind gradients
   affecting the local speed of sound. This is similar to how light bends as it goes through a denser medium, such as air to water.
   By flying at a sufficiently high altitude and at the correct speed for current atmospheric conditions, XB-1 can ensure that its
   sonic boom created will never reach the ground.

How are Low Boom and Boomless Cruise different?

          
   The X-59’s unique shape allows it to reduce the decibels of the sonic boom produced, effectively reducing the booms strength 
   to the sound of slamming a car door. Boomless cruise is different to low boom as the XB-1 still produces a normal sonic
   boom but the sound waves are directed away from the ground. Boomless cruise also doesn't need extensive aircraft shaping as 
   seen in the X-59, making it the more accessible technology to introduce to current and new aircraft.
